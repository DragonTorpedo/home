The Golden Rules

I

Structure with XML

Claude is trained to parse XML. Wrapping your inputs in tags is the single most effective way to prevent hallucinations and keep focus locked.

Use these <context> for reference material  ·  <instructions> for commands  ·  <thinking> to surface reasoning  ·  <answer> for the final output
II

Tell it what to do

Negative constraints confuse the attention mechanism. Replace every "don't" with a direct instruction about what you actually want.

Weak → Strong ✗ "Don't write fluff and don't use bullet points."
✓ "Write in flowing prose paragraphs. Start directly with the core argument."
III

Let it think

Opus 4.8 and Sonnet 4.6 use Adaptive Thinking — they determine their own reasoning depth. Stop prescribing steps. Tell Claude to "think thoroughly" and move the Effort slider. Its native path beats yours.

IV

Build the sandbox

A question is the weakest prompt. Stack constraints to define exactly the space the model operates inside.

Constraint stack Persona → "Act as a Senior Staff Engineer reviewing a junior's PR."
Audience → "The reader is the executive board. No technical background."
Format → "Output valid JSON: risk_level, summary, mitigation_steps."

Advanced Workflows

Rubber Duck Debugging

Pasting an error and asking for a fix guarantees you learn nothing. Make Claude mentor you instead.

"Here is the code and the error. Do NOT give me the fix. Ask me 1–2 guiding questions that help me find the root cause myself."

Self-Correction Loop

Models drop constraints in long outputs. Force an internal audit before the answer reaches you.

"Draft the report. Before outputting, verify against these three criteria. If any fail, rewrite until all pass."

Knowledge Bridge

Learning something new? Anchor it to what you already know. Skip the textbook, get the 20% that covers 80% of your needs.

"I know Pandas deeply. Explain [New Tool] by mapping its concepts directly to Pandas functions I already use."
